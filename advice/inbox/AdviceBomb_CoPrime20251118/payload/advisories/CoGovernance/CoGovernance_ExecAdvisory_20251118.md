# Executive & Board Advisory  -  Using CoCivium Governance Patterns

Origin-Session: CoCivia20251118 / CoCivium governance discussion
Intended-For: CoPrime20251118 (a.k.a. Co1)
Version: 2025-11-18

## 1. Purpose

This document reframes CoCivium’s governance ideas for outside
executives, boards, and sponsors who may encounter CoCivium, or who
may want to borrow its patterns.

It is intentionally written in plain, executive-facing language but
is compatible with the internal Governance Atlas and Insight Pairs.


## 2. Core message for CEOs / boards

1. **Titles are tools, not thrones.**  
   Authority in CoCivium is temporary, transparent, and tied to
   specific initiatives.

2. **Decisions are backed by receipts.**  
   Every important decision has: a proposal card, tests, logs,
   and a clear rollback plan.

3. **Risk is managed with small, reversible moves.**  
   CoCivium prefers reversible experiments over one-way bets.

4. **Disputes are handled by consent, mediation, and sortition.**  
   When conflict arises, narrow panels decide on bounded questions
   with documented evidence.


## 3. Exportable governance patterns

The following patterns can be adopted by external organizations
without importing the full CoCivium stack.

### 3.1 Initiative Cards

Instead of ad hoc decisions, executives can require that each major
initiative have a one-page card:

- Objective, scope, and success metrics.
- Risks and rollback strategy.
- Tests and evidence to collect.
- Requested temporary authority (if any).
- Support thresholds (how many reviewers, what expertise).

This reduces ambiguity and ensures that “approval” is tied to clear
commitments and safeguards.


### 3.2 Sortition Panels

For thorny decisions with high stakes or strong disagreement, boards
can adopt a simple sortition (jury-by-lot) pattern:

- Randomly select a small panel from an eligible pool.
- Ensure basic conflict-of-interest checks.
- Ask the panel to decide a narrow, time-bounded question.
- Record the decision and reasoning, and revisit at a set date.


### 3.3 Safety Pauses

Organizations can formalize safety pauses as a recognized tool:

- Any trusted role can request a pause on a specific initiative.
- The pause is time-boxed and must be documented with a short
  rationale.
- A review group decides whether to lift, extend, or roll back.


### 3.4 Evidence Packs

Before approving or reviewing major initiatives, boards can demand
“evidence packs” that include:

- The initiative card.
- Test results and logs.
- Impact and risk assessments.
- Previous related decisions.


## 4. Weaknesses and considerations

- Leaders may feel constrained by explicit thresholds and panels,
  especially if they are used to informal decision-making.
- Sortition can seem strange to organizations accustomed to purely
  hierarchical authority.
- Without strong communication, these mechanisms can be perceived
  as bureaucracy rather than safety and clarity.
